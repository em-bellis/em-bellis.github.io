Emily S. Bellis, Ph.D.

Welcome! I am an Assistant Professor of Bioinformatics at Arkansas State University in the Department of Computer Science. I study complex organism interactions with abiotic and biotic environments and how these interactions change across space and over time. I take inspiration from evolutionary genomics, spatial ecology, and machine learning and am particularly interested in novel intersections of these fields to address problems relevant to food security and ecosystem health in a rapidly changing climate.

I currently have paid opportunities for undergraduate students and a Ph.D. Student (through the Molecular Biosciences or Environmental Sciences programs) or Master's Student to join the lab. Please contact Emily for more information on projects of potential mutual interest!

Research

Adaptation to Environment

Understanding the genetic basis of adaptation to environment is critical for predicting and mitigating impact of rapidly changing climates on ecosystems and human societies. A primary goal of the lab is to integrate genomic, phenotypic, and environmental data to identify genetic variation important for adaptation to biotic and abiotic environments. Previous work in marine systems supports a role for adaptation to local environments via selection on the ability to associate with distinct microbial symbionts (Bellis et al. 2018, Bellis & Denver 2017). We are continuing a special focus on adaptation to biotic environments through work on coevolutionary genomics of the parasitic witchweed Striga hermonthica and its cereal hosts, which include sorghum, rice, millet, and maize. S. hermonthica is a devastating constraint to food security across much of Africa, and we are leveraging ecological and evolutionary genomic perspectives to help inform sorghum breeding and weed management in smallholder farming systems. We are also developing novel approaches for improved understanding and prediction of plant-environment interactions, through integration of machine learning and evolutionary genomic modeling frameworks.

Conflict & Cooperation

The dynamics of interactions within and between organisms and their environment is a central component of the complexity of life, from organelles that power plant and animal cells, to beneficial microbes that help lay the foundation of coral reef ecosystems, to damaging parasites of agriculture. Previous work in this area has focused on characterizing evolutionary dynamics of selfish genetic elements in nuclear and organellar genomes of invertebrates including sea anemones, water fleas, and nematodes. Future and ongoing projects will focus on understanding how interactions between species shape dynamics of intragenomic conflict & cooperation in weedy & domesticated plants.

Emily Bellis, Ph.D.

Emily is from Arkansas and completed a B.S. in Genetics & Biochemistry at Texas A&M University (College Station, TX) in 2010 and a Ph.D. in Integrative Biology in 2017 at Oregon State University (Corvallis, OR), working with Dee Denver. After a short postdoc on population genomics of transposable elements with Sarah Schaack at Reed College (Portland, OR), she worked with Jesse Lasky and Claude dePamphilis at Penn State (State College, PA) as an NSF Postdoctoral Research Fellow in Biology through the National Plant Genome Initiative. She joined A-State in late 2019 as a Research Assistant Professor of Bioinformatics. In her spare time, she enjoys hiking, knitting, and attempting unsuccessfully to tire out her energetic Border Collie/Aussie, Pascal.
